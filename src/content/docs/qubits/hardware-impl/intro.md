The biggest strength of digital classical computing, compared to analog computing or photonic computing, is the ease by which you can implement classical computers. All bits toggle between 0 and 1, so there is a large cushion: for example, if we implement a bit as a lightbulb, even a dim lightbulb can represent a 1 position when turned on and 0 when turned off. As a result of this simplicity and relative ease, humans have driven down the size and cost of transistors so small that we can easily pack billions of them into tiny packages. 

Qubits are comparatively extremely difficult to work with. At a theoretical level, qubits must exhibit superposition and entanglement. The things in nature that exhibit this behavior naturally are extremely small: individual electrons, individual photons, majorana particles, etc. They are difficult to procure, control, observe, and store - often at super low temperatures (fun fact: the big golden structures you probably associate with quantum computers are actually extremely cold refrigerators). 

Altogether, it is not particularly clear what the “obvious” or “winning” physical qubits that will allow for scalable, cheap quantum computers are. However, scientists from across the world working in government, academia, and the private sector have developed quite a few promising platforms.
