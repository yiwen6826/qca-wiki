---
title: Photonic Qubits
---
### How They Work
Photonic qubits store quantum information encoded in photons, which are the fundamental particles of light. There are several ways to encode this information. Common methods include polarization (horizontal/vertical), path (different spatial modes of the photon), time-bin (early/late arrival times), and frequency. In polarization encoding, wave plates act as single-qubit gates by rotating the photon’s polarization state, and beam splitters can create superpositions of spatial paths by combination of transmitting or reflecting. Companies such as PsiQuantum and Xanadu are currently developing quantum systems based on photonic qubits.

### Pros
Unlike superconducting qubits, they do not require cryogenic temperatures to work. They can function at room temperatures which eliminates the need for expensive cooling systems. Also, photons are naturally resilient to outside noise/environmental interactions which leads to longer coherence times. Since fiber optics transmit data through pulses of photons, photonic qubits are compatible with fiber optics which leads to better scalability and the ability to travel long distances through fiber optic cables. Because of this compatibility with fiber optics and the fact that photons travel at the speed of light, they are the ideal candidates for developing quantum communication and networks.
### Cons
Even though photonic qubits can maintain longer coherence times compared to other types of qubits, photons can still be lost. Unlike other qubits which just lose quantum properties through environmental interactions, photons primarily disappear due to transmission loss in optical fibers, imperfect coupling between components, and device imperfections. Also, because they interact very weakly with the environment, it is challenging to implement two qubit gates and make photonic qubits work together.
